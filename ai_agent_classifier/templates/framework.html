Research Framework

A 3-Dimensional Classification System
for AI Agents in the Investment Industry

Financial markets are complex adaptive systems — and AI tools are proliferating faster than the vocabulary to describe them. This framework gives investment managers, developers, and regulators a shared language to evaluate any AI agent across three structural dimensions: what environment it operates in, where it sits in the investment process, and what competitive edge it enables.

0 The Problem

Why a classification system?

Without a shared taxonomy, investment firms risk deploying the wrong AI tool for the wrong context — over-automating decisions that require human judgment, or under-utilizing AI where it could create durable edge. Three root problems motivate this work:

Inconsistent Taxonomy

There remains no consistent way to compare or categorize the roles, capabilities, and strategic potential of AI tools as they are progressively integrated into investment workflows.

Poor Strategic Deployment

Firms lack the knowledge to use AI strategically to create business value, optimize decisions, and maximize the asymmetry between risk and return.

Lack of Scalability & Interpretability

Addressing ethical implications requires Explainable AI (XAI) to demystify "black-box" models, foster transparency, and build trust in the investment process.

Regulatory & Ethical Ambiguity

The industry lacks robust governance frameworks to effectively address algorithmic bias, operationalize AI ethics, and ensure responsible integration.

1 The Framework

Three axes. One coordinate system.

Every AI agent in the investment industry can be located in a three-dimensional space. Together, these dimensions define not just what an agent does — but whether it belongs there.

Axis Y — Exogenous
Market Complexity

How predictable is the environment in which this agent operates? Ranges from fully quantifiable risk to genuine unknown unknowns — and the right AI approach is radically different at each level.

Axis X — Endogenous
Investment Process Stage

Where in the investment value chain does this agent contribute? From originating ideas to reporting outcomes to stakeholders, each stage has distinct information needs and decision stakes.

Axis Z — Bridge
Comparative Advantage

What type of edge does this agent help create or protect? Informational, analytical, and behavioral advantages differ in durability and in how vulnerable they are to erosion over time.

2 Axis Y — Market Complexity

The Swan Spectrum: from risk to radical uncertainty

Drawing on Knight (1921), Taleb (2012), and Gigerenzer (2001), the framework maps market environments onto four complexity tiers. The critical insight: as uncertainty increases, simpler AI approaches outperform optimized ones. Fitting a sophisticated model to a Black Swan environment is not just ineffective — it is epistemologically wrong.

← Risk (quantifiable) Uncertainty (irreducible) →
🦢
White Swan
Known knowns · Measurable risk

Options and probabilities are known — there is an objective basis for deriving outcome probabilities. Statistical algorithms and probabilistic models are appropriate. Research consistently shows they outperform human experts in this regime.

E.g. Options pricing, index replication, execution algorithms, compliance rule-checking.

🩶
Light Grey Swan
Known unknowns · Noisy search

The probability distribution is theoretically known but hard to estimate due to data noise. Many options are presented as available, requiring substantial search across a noisy solution space. Optimization heuristics — simplified models that drop intractable constraints — are preferred.

E.g. Factor models, portfolio optimization, NLP-based screening of large document sets.

🌫️
Dark Grey Swan
Known unknowns · Scarce data

Little information is available. Getting the right causal direction matters more than the weights. The appropriate method is a hypothetico-deductive model based on logic, complemented by scenario analysis and Bayesian updating as new evidence emerges.

E.g. Macro regime analysis, geopolitical risk models, early-stage credit assessment.

🖤
Black Swan
Unknown unknowns · Irreducible

The odds of rare events are not computable. Robustness beats optimality. Outsourcing responsibilities to a mathematical formula is not only ethically wrong, but also epistemologically flawed. Simple heuristics and human judgment must complement or replace models.

E.g. Tail-risk stress testing, crisis response, liquidity freeze scenarios.

Key implication: AI tools appropriate for White Swan environments (statistical optimization) become actively dangerous in Black Swan contexts. The framework forces explicit acknowledgment of which regime an agent is deployed into.
3 Axis X — Investment Process Value Chain

The 7 stages of the investment process

Although investment strategies differ widely, the process follows a consistent sequence across firms. Each stage has distinct information needs, human-AI interaction patterns, and regulatory exposure. An AI agent may contribute to one or several stages — and its appropriateness differs at each.

💡
Idea Generation
Stage 1

Identifying investment opportunities. Top-down screening or bottom-up discovery. AI can expand the universe searched and surface non-obvious signals.

🔬
Idea Assessment
Stage 2

Evaluating opportunities on risk-return merit against portfolio constraints. AI supports deeper, faster fundamental and quantitative analysis.

⚡
Decision Point
Stage 3

Formal capital commitment. The highest-stakes stage. AI should support — not replace — human judgment, particularly in uncertain environments.

🚀
Execution
Stage 4

Trading to establish positions. AI optimizes across venues, minimizes market impact, and manages execution risk through algorithmic strategies.

📡
Monitoring
Stage 5

Continuous assessment of position alignment with objectives. AI tracks drift, detects thesis-breaking signals, and flags rebalancing needs.

🛡️
Compliance
Stage 6

Ensuring regulatory and mandate adherence throughout the lifecycle. AI automates rule-checking, audit trail generation, and regulatory reporting.

🤝
Stakeholder Mgmt
Stage 7

Communicating rationale and performance to clients. AI generates explainable narratives, personalized reports, and risk summaries at scale.

Key implication: AI impact is not uniform across stages. Execution and Compliance are high-AI-fit (White Swan, deterministic rules). The Decision Point demands the most caution — autonomy here depends critically on the complexity tier.
4 Axis Z — Comparative Advantage

Three types of edge — and their durability

Sustained investment outperformance requires a durable advantage over equally skilled competitors. Drawing on Fuller (1998) and the CAB framework (Wierckx, 2021), the taxonomy identifies three types that differ fundamentally in how quickly AI erodes or amplifies them.

📊
Informational Advantage
Durability: Low · Erosion: Fast

Possessing material knowledge not yet reflected in prices — earlier news access, proprietary datasets, alternative data. Barriers to acquiring this advantage have dropped dramatically due to web scraping, alternative data, and faster data feeds, making informational edges commoditize rapidly. Once information is disseminated, the advantage vanishes.

AI role: Web scraping, news sentiment, earnings transcripts NLP, satellite data processing.

🧠
Analytical Advantage
Durability: Medium · Erosion: Gradual

Superior synthesis of complex or fragmented public information — better models, deeper research, sharper inference. Requires significant intellectual capital and creates meaningful barriers to entry. However, as LLMs and ML improve, the analytical moat is narrowing across the industry.

AI role: Multi-factor modeling, financial statement parsing, sector-specific LLMs, portfolio optimization.

🎯
Behavioral Advantage
Durability: High · Erosion: Slow

Capitalizing on the persistent irrationalities of other market participants. Cognitive biases are deeply ingrained — knowing about a bias does not equate to overcoming it. This makes behavioral inefficiencies remarkably durable. AI can both detect behavioral patterns in markets and help investors enforce their own discipline.

AI role: Bias detection in decision workflows, systematic discipline enforcement, sentiment contrarianism.

Analytical

5 Supporting Dimensions

Autonomy & Evaluation Parameters

Beyond the three primary axes, each agent is characterized by where it sits on the autonomy spectrum — from low-autonomy copilots that surface information for human review to fully autonomous agents that act without real-time oversight — and by a set of evaluation parameters that determine its operational fit within an enterprise investment workflow.

Autonomy Level
Low Autonomy
Decision Support

Surfaces information and analysis; all decisions require human sign-off. Examples: copilots, dashboards, search tools.

Medium Autonomy
Assisted Execution

Executes within defined parameters but escalates edge cases to humans. Examples: rule-based execution algos, compliance screeners.

High Autonomy
Supervised Agency

Acts independently within broad constraints; humans monitor and can intervene. Examples: systematic quant strategies, robo-advisors.

Fully Autonomous
End-to-End Agency

Operates without real-time human oversight. The most consequential category — regulatory alignment and robustness criteria are most demanding here.

Evaluation Parameters
Learning Capability

How the agent is trained and updated, ranging from centrally engineered domain data with controlled retrieval to continuous, unsupervised online learning.

Interaction Mode

The mechanism of user engagement, such as in-platform natural-language queries, API integrations, or programmatic data feeds.

Regulatory Compliance

The agent's alignment with enterprise guardrails, including scoped data access, timestamped auditability, and content filtering.

Decision Function

The specific role played by the agent, whether generating signals, supporting decision-productivity, or acting with execution authority.

7 Reading the Matrix

How to use this classification system

Any AI agent in the investment industry can be pinned to a coordinate in this space. That coordinate answers a practical question: is this the right tool, in the right place, for the right kind of problem?

1
Identify the complexity tier

Is the problem fundamentally quantifiable (White) or irreducibly uncertain (Black)? This determines which class of AI approach is epistemologically valid.

2
Map the process stage(s)

Where does the agent intervene in the value chain? An agent can span multiple stages — but each stage has different autonomy tolerances and regulatory exposure.

3
Assign the comparative advantage

Which type of edge does this agent support — or create? Align AI deployment with the firm's actual competitive strategy, not just available tooling.

4
Calibrate autonomy to context

Higher complexity demands lower autonomy. A fully autonomous agent in a Black Swan environment is misclassified — or misdeployed. The matrix makes this visible.

5
Assess governance fit

Regulators and compliance teams can use the coordinate to determine oversight requirements: auditability, explainability, human-in-the-loop thresholds.
